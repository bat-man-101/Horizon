## [Supreme Court: Geofence warrants need constitutional protections](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jun/29/supreme-court-geofence-warrants-case-decision) ⭐️ 9.0/10

The US Supreme Court ruled that geofence warrants, which compel tech companies to provide location data for all devices within a specific area, must comply with Fourth Amendment protections against unreasonable searches and seizures. This landmark decision significantly limits warrantless bulk location data collection by law enforcement, strengthening digital privacy rights for millions of smartphone users and setting a precedent for future surveillance technologies. The ruling arose from a bank robbery case where Google provided location data for 19 devices near the crime scene; the Court held that such warrants require probable cause and particularity, similar to traditional search warrants.

**Background**: A geofence warrant is a court order that requires a company like Google to identify all mobile devices within a defined geographic area during a specific time. The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches, but its application to digital location data has been debated. The Supreme Court's 2026 decision extends earlier rulings like Carpenter v. United States to geofence warrants.

The family includes 9B Dense, 31B Dense, 35B MoE, and 397B MoE variants, built on Gemma 4 and Qwen 3.5. Ornith-1.0 is significant because it brings state-of-the-art agentic coding capabilities to the open-source community with a permissive MIT license, enabling developers to run powerful coding agents locally. This could accelerate the adoption of AI-assisted software development and reduce reliance on proprietary models. The model uses a self-scaffolding approach that requires no external components or architectural modifications, converting implicit reasoning into explicit textual decompositions. Initial tests show it can proficiently handle multiple tool calls in an agent harness, running at 103 tokens/second for image generation.

This reframing shifts the power dynamic in human-AI collaboration, emphasizing human agency and reviewability, which is crucial for building trust and safety in AI-assisted software development. Udell specifically warns against 'unreviewable pull requests' generated by agents, advocating for transparent, human-led processes where agents contribute within established workflows.

**Background**: The phrase 'human in the loop' traditionally describes systems where humans supervise AI decisions. However, critics argue it implies humans are secondary to automated processes. Udell's 'agent in the loop' flips this, positioning humans as the primary decision-makers who invite AI agents to assist, not replace, their work.
